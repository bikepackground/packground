# BIKE BAIKAL: WINTER BIKEPACKING IN SIBERIA

**Last updated:** February 2018  
*Please help keep this information up to date! (Contact via Instagram [@bikepackground](https://www.instagram.com/bikepackground))*

## OVERVIEW

Lake Baikal, the "Pearl of Russia," is the world’s second-largest freshwater lake. During the brutal Siberian winter, it transforms into a massive expanse of ice over a meter thick—the ultimate stage for a winter bikepacking expedition. 

This is an adventure defined by extremes: temperatures can plummet to -30°C, and the terrain is ever-shifting. While smooth "black ice" rides like a paved road with studded tires, the lake is a living entity that cracks, moans, and throws up obstacles like pressure ridges and deep snow drifts. Success requires meticulous preparation, iron nerves, and significant winter cycling experience.

## ROUTE & TERRAIN

**The Journey:** Starts in Listvyanka (70 km from Irkutsk). Most cyclists take a bus to the shore to avoid traffic. From there, the route follows the western coast 600 km north to Severobaikalsk (connected to the Trans-Siberian Railway).

**Ice Dynamics:** You will encounter active cracks, especially around capes and between Olkhon Island and the Holy Nose peninsula. Look for areas where ice plates push together frontally to cross. Avoid water on the ice or plates that have submerged. Fine ice crystals on newly frozen water indicate unstable ice.

**Navigation:** There is no fixed GPS track; the "road" changes daily. Navigation is done "cape to cape." The eastern shore is typically snowier; following existing vehicle tracks is often the most efficient path.

## INFRASTRUCTURE & LOGISTICS

**Accommodation:** Guesthouses exist in Listvyanka, Bukuldeika, Khuzhir (Olkhon), and Severobaikalsk. You may find shelter with park guards or in remote saunas. However, a four-season winter tent and bivouac system are mandatory.

**Supplies:** Irkutsk and Severobaikalsk have large supermarkets. Khuzhir is the last major resupply point. Carry at least six days of food, as daily distances often drop to 50 km or less due to cold and short days.

**Essential Gear:** Ice screws instead of tent pegs, a small axe for firewood or melting ice, and high-quality thermos flasks.

**Money:** Cash only (Rubles) outside of large supermarkets. ATMs are only in Irkutsk and Severobaikalsk.

## SAFETY & TIMING

**Timing:** Late January to late April, with mid-February to early March being the prime window. Note that climate change makes this increasingly unpredictable.

**Resources:** Check daily satellite imagery for open water on http://sputnik.irk.ru/main/baikal/last/. 
Contact: Youri Nemirovsky (baikalcomplex.com) is an excellent English-speaking resource.

**Emergency:** EMERCOM helpline: +7 (499) 216 99 99. Satellite messengers (SPOT/Garmin) are highly recommended.

## DIFFICULTY

This expedition depends on physical fitness, mental resilience, and luck regarding the weather. Crossing Baikal unsupported is one of the most grueling yet visually stunning experiences a cyclist can have. As they say in Russia: You don't get a pearl for free. Äto Baikal! (That's Baikal!)
