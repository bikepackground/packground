# PIONEER TRAIL: BIKEPACKING THE WILD WEST WONDERS OF THE USA

**Last updated:** May 2016

## OVERVIEW

Utah is world-renowned for its surreal rock formations, slot canyons, and vast desert landscapes. The Pioneer Trail is designed for those looking to discover this iconic region off the beaten path. 

A true highlight is the Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument, a pristine wilderness with minimal regulations. The route traverses this monument three times via distinct dirt tracks, eventually linking up with the Burr Trail through Capitol Reef and concluding in the Lockhart Basin—a hidden gem in Canyonlands National Park that leads you along the Colorado River to the world-famous slickrock of Moab.

## ROUTE & TERRAIN

**Grand Staircase-Escalante:** The trail begins in Cannonville and explores the monument via three unique routes: the sandy Cottonwood Canyon Road, the steep Smoky Mountain Road, and the historic Hole-in-the-Rock Road. 
*Tip: Don't miss Zebra Slot Canyon or the view from Alstrom Point high above Lake Powell. Though Alstrom Point is a 20 km dead-end, the sunset view is worth every pedal stroke.*

**The Burr Trail & Beyond:** From Escalante, you follow the road to Boulder and turn onto the Burr Trail. This historic cattle route features a stunning paved canyon section before the famous unpaved switchbacks drop you into Capitol Reef National Park. After crossing Lake Powell via the Bullfrog Marina ferry, the route heads toward Blanding and Monticello.

**Lockhart Basin:** Near the Needles district of Canyonlands, the route enters the Lockhart Basin. This is a true "Western" backdrop—lonely, rugged, and free of the permit requirements found on the neighboring White Rim Trail. The ATV track is sandy but mostly ridable, offering a fantastic, remote approach to Moab.

## INFRASTRUCTURE & LOGISTICS

**Permits:** While wild camping is allowed throughout the Escalante Monument, you must obtain a free backcountry permit from the BLM visitor centers (Cannonville, Big Water, or Escalante). In general, "stealth camping" on public lands in the US is easy and legal.

**Resupply:** Towns along the route offer motels, developed campgrounds, and general stores. For a larger selection, you can hitchhike from Big Water to the supermarkets in Page, Arizona.

**Water – The Critical Factor:** Reliable sources are scarce. 
* **Warning:** Avoid Collet Canyon Creek; despite its clarity, it contains harmful minerals that can cause illness. Stick to visitor centers for refills. 
* **Filtration:** Larger rivers like the Colorado contain high levels of sediment that will quickly clog filters; chemical treatment or boiling is necessary.

## DIFFICULTY

With 59% pavement, the Pioneer Trail is a moderate route, but its desert environment adds significant challenge.

**Climate:** Avoid the scorching summer months; Spring (April/May) and Autumn are the only suitable windows. Dirt roads become impassable "death mud" after rain. Check with visitor centers for conditions.

**Isolation:** There is no mobile coverage in the Monument or Lockhart Basin. You must be self-sufficient and experienced in wilderness travel. A minimum tire width of 2.0" is mandatory for sandy and rocky sections.
